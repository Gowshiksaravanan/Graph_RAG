Interview with Dr. Karen Osei - Chief Compliance Officer, NovaTech Industries
Date: April 2, 2024
Location: NovaTech Headquarters, Chicago, Illinois

I am Dr. Karen Osei, Chief Compliance Officer at NovaTech Industries. I hold a JD from Georgetown University Law Center and a PhD in Regulatory Science from Johns Hopkins University. I joined NovaTech in 2019 after twelve years at Honeywell International where I served as Senior Director of Global Trade Compliance. My team of 18 compliance professionals manages regulatory affairs, trade compliance, environmental compliance, product safety certifications, and ethics and anti-corruption programs across all NovaTech operations globally.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: What are the main regulatory frameworks NovaTech must comply with?

NovaTech operates in a complex regulatory environment spanning multiple jurisdictions. In the United States, our primary regulatory obligations include compliance with the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) under the Department of Commerce. Several of our ProLine and EdgeSense products contain controlled technology classified under ECCN 3A001 and 3A002. This means we need export licenses for shipments to certain countries, particularly China, Russia, and countries subject to comprehensive sanctions. Our export compliance manager, Daniel Park, reviews every international order against the BIS Entity List, the Denied Persons List, and OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list.

We also comply with the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) for a small subset of our ProLine products that have dual-use military applications. These products are manufactured exclusively at our Detroit plant under a restricted access area supervised by Robert Kim. Only US persons are allowed access to ITAR-controlled technical data and manufacturing areas.

In Europe, NovaTech Europe GmbH must comply with the EU's CE marking directives, including the Low Voltage Directive 2014/35/EU, the Electromagnetic Compatibility Directive 2014/30/EU, and the Machinery Directive 2006/42/EC. Our European regulatory affairs manager, Dr. Claudia Richter, based in Munich, manages all CE certification processes. She works with TÜV Rheinland as our notified body for product testing and certification.

The EU's REACH regulation (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals) requires us to track and disclose all substances of very high concern (SVHC) in our products. Our materials compliance specialist, Yuki Tanabe, maintains a materials declaration database of over 4,000 components. In 2023, we discovered that a capacitor from one of our tier-two suppliers, Nichicon Corporation, contained a lead compound that exceeded the RoHS Directive threshold of 0.1 percent by weight. Yuki escalated this to Lisa Yamamoto in procurement, and we worked with Nichicon to switch to a compliant alternative within 60 days. The non-compliant inventory — approximately 12,000 units worth 340,000 dollars — was quarantined and returned to the supplier.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How do you manage compliance in the China operations?

China is our most complex compliance environment. The Shenzhen plant manager, Helen Wu, works closely with our China compliance lead, Vincent Liu, who is based in our Shanghai legal office. China compliance covers several areas.

First, the China Cybersecurity Law and the Data Security Law of 2021 restrict cross-border transfer of certain data. Since our EdgeSense IoT products collect sensor data from Chinese customers, we must store this data locally on servers hosted by Alibaba Cloud within mainland China. Our CTO Raj Patel implemented a data localization architecture that keeps Chinese customer data separate from our global EdgeSense Cloud Platform hosted on AWS in Virginia. The annual cost of maintaining this parallel infrastructure is approximately 800,000 dollars.

Second, China's export control law, effective December 2020, requires us to obtain permits for certain technology transfers from NovaTech China Ltd. to our US operations. This has created tension with our global R&D sharing practices. Our General Counsel, Patricia Mbeki, negotiated a technology transfer framework with Chinese authorities that allows controlled sharing of non-sensitive product improvement data.

Third, the Dual Circulation Strategy and Made in China 2025 policies have increased pressure on our Shenzhen operation to source more components domestically. Currently, about 45 percent of EdgeSense components are sourced from Chinese suppliers including Wingtech Technology for semiconductor packaging and Luxshare Precision for connectors. The Chinese government provides tax incentives through the Shenzhen High-Tech Industrial Development Zone where our plant is located, but these incentives are conditional on maintaining a minimum 40 percent local content ratio.

Fourth, US-China trade tensions have directly impacted our operations. The Section 301 tariffs imposed 25 percent duties on certain components imported from China to the US. Our CFO Michael Torres and I worked with our trade counsel at Baker McKenzie to structure our supply chain so that EdgeSense products destined for the US market are assembled in Guadalajara using a mix of Chinese and non-Chinese components, qualifying for USMCA preferential treatment. This restructuring, called Project Phoenix internally, took nine months and reduced our tariff exposure by approximately 6.2 million dollars annually.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: What environmental and sustainability regulations affect NovaTech?

Environmental compliance is increasingly critical. In the US, our Detroit plant is regulated by the EPA under the Clean Air Act and the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) for hazardous waste management. We generate approximately 15 metric tons of hazardous waste annually from our machining and plating operations. This waste is handled by Clean Harbors, our licensed hazardous waste disposal contractor, under EPA manifest tracking. Our Environmental Health and Safety manager at Detroit, Frank Morrison, conducts quarterly waste audits and maintains our Tier II chemical inventory reports for SARA Title III.

The EU's WEEE Directive (Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment) requires us to finance the collection, treatment, and recycling of our electronic products sold in Europe. We are registered as a producer in all 27 EU member states through a compliance scheme managed by European Recycling Platform (ERP). Our WEEE obligations cost approximately 1.1 million euros annually. Dr. Claudia Richter manages our WEEE registration and reporting.

California's Proposition 65 requires warning labels on products containing chemicals known to cause cancer or reproductive harm. Several of our products contain small amounts of lead solder (in legacy components) and nickel compounds that trigger Prop 65 obligations. Our product labeling specialist, Maria Santos, ensures all products shipped to California carry appropriate warnings. We are actively working with our engineering team to eliminate Prop 65-listed substances from all product lines by 2026 as part of our Green Product Initiative, sponsored by our CEO, Thomas Wright.

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which entered its transitional phase in October 2023, requires importers of certain goods into the EU to report embedded carbon emissions. While our products are not currently in scope for CBAM Phase 1, our VP of Pricing Sarah Mitchell and I are monitoring the planned expansion to include electronics and electrical equipment, expected in Phase 3 around 2028. Dr. Priya Krishnamurthy's analytics team is building a carbon footprint calculator for our products that will integrate with our SAP system to track Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions across the supply chain.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How do you handle anti-corruption and ethics compliance?

NovaTech has a comprehensive anti-corruption program required by the US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and the UK Bribery Act 2010. We operate in countries with high corruption risk indices — Mexico, China, India, and parts of Southeast Asia. Our ethics and compliance training is mandatory for all employees globally and is delivered through our SAP SuccessFactors learning management system. Completion rates must be 100 percent; last year we achieved 98.7 percent, with the remaining 1.3 percent completing within the grace period.

We have a third-party due diligence program for all distributors, agents, and intermediaries. Before onboarding Tata AutoComp as our India distribution partner, we conducted a six-month due diligence process through our external investigator, Kroll Associates. The investigation covered beneficial ownership, litigation history, government connections, and PEP (Politically Exposed Person) screening. Our legal team, led by General Counsel Patricia Mbeki, reviewed the findings before approving the partnership.

Our confidential ethics hotline, operated by NAVEX Global, received 42 reports in fiscal year 2023. Of these, 12 were substantiated after investigation. The most significant case involved a procurement buyer at the Guadalajara plant who was accepting gifts exceeding our 50-dollar threshold from a tier-two supplier, Electrocomponentes de Mexico. The buyer was terminated, the supplier was placed on probation for six months, and we conducted an audit of all procurement transactions with that supplier over the prior 24 months. No additional violations were found. Carlos Mendoza, the Guadalajara plant manager, reinforced ethics training for all procurement staff following this incident.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How do you see the regulatory landscape evolving?

Several emerging regulations will significantly impact NovaTech. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) will require NovaTech Europe GmbH to publish detailed sustainability reports starting fiscal year 2025, covering ESG metrics including carbon emissions, labor practices in our supply chain, and governance structures. Our sustainability director, Amara Johnson, is leading CSRD readiness with a budget of 1.5 million dollars.

The EU AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024, classifies certain AI-powered industrial automation systems as high-risk. Our EdgeSense products that use machine learning for predictive maintenance may fall under this classification. Raj Patel and I have established a joint AI governance committee to assess which products require conformity assessments and additional documentation under the Act.

The US CHIPS and Science Act provides incentives for domestic semiconductor manufacturing and may reduce our dependency on foreign semiconductor suppliers over the next five to ten years. Additionally, the proposed US federal data privacy legislation, if passed, would create new obligations around how our EdgeSense platform collects and processes customer data domestically, similar to what we already comply with under GDPR in Europe and China's PIPL. Our Data Protection Officer, Jennifer Walsh, is tracking these developments and preparing readiness assessments.
