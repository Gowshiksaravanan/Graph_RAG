Interview with James Rodriguez - SVP of Supply Chain Operations, NovaTech Industries
Date: March 22, 2024
Location: NovaTech Detroit Manufacturing Plant, Detroit, Michigan

I'm James Rodriguez, Senior Vice President of Supply Chain Operations at NovaTech Industries. I've been with the company for eleven years, the last four in this role. I oversee all manufacturing operations, logistics, warehousing, procurement, and supplier relationship management across our three plants.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: Can you describe NovaTech's manufacturing footprint and what each plant produces?

Our Detroit plant is the oldest, built in 2003, and it's our primary facility for the ProLine heavy industrial automation series. The plant has 340 employees and runs two shifts. Production capacity is approximately 8,000 ProLine units per month. The plant manager is Robert Kim, and he reports directly to me. Detroit handles all the precision machining, assembly, and quality testing for ProLine. We invested 18 million dollars in a new robotic welding line from FANUC in 2022, which increased throughput by 22 percent.

The Guadalajara plant was established in 2011 and is focused on the FlexCore series and the new GreenDrive series. It has 520 employees across three shifts. The plant manager is Carlos Mendoza. Guadalajara was chosen for FlexCore production because of lower labor costs and proximity to the US market via the USMCA trade agreement. The plant handles PCB assembly using equipment from Juki Corporation and Yamaha Motor, final assembly, and burn-in testing. Monthly capacity is around 15,000 FlexCore units and 3,000 GreenDrive units. We recently added a clean room facility for GreenDrive production because the energy-efficient motor controllers require more precise assembly tolerances.

The Shenzhen plant opened in 2016 specifically for the EdgeSense IoT product line. It has 280 employees and is managed by Helen Wu. Shenzhen is ideal for EdgeSense because the local ecosystem provides access to electronic component suppliers, rapid prototyping services, and specialized SMT assembly expertise. The plant produces approximately 50,000 EdgeSense sensor modules per month. About 60 percent of EdgeSense production serves the Asia-Pacific market, with the remainder shipped to North America and Europe through our Hong Kong logistics hub.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How is your supply chain structured from raw materials to delivery?

Our supply chain has five main stages. First is procurement, managed by Lisa Yamamoto, our procurement director. She leads a team of 25 buyers who manage relationships with over 200 tier-one suppliers. We categorize suppliers into three tiers. Tier-one strategic suppliers include Texas Instruments for microcontrollers, Freeport-McMoRan for copper, Alcoa for aluminum, Murata Manufacturing for capacitors, and TE Connectivity for connectors. These suppliers are managed under long-term contracts of three to five years with volume commitments and price escalation clauses.

Tier-two preferred suppliers include companies like Molex for specialized connectors, Vishay Intertechnology for resistors, and Nidec Corporation for motors used in GreenDrive. Tier-three transactional suppliers cover commodity parts like fasteners, packaging materials, and general-purpose components. We source from distributors like Arrow Electronics and Avnet for tier-three items. Our total annual procurement spend is approximately 480 million dollars.

The second stage is inbound logistics. Raw materials and components are shipped to our plants via a combination of ocean freight, air freight, and ground transportation. For ocean freight, we primarily use Maersk and MSC Mediterranean Shipping Company. Our customs brokerage is handled by C.H. Robinson. Inbound logistics from Asia to Detroit or Guadalajara typically takes 28 to 35 days by ocean. For urgent shipments, we use DHL Express air freight, which reduces transit to 3 to 5 days but at five to eight times the cost.

Third is manufacturing, which I described with the three plants. Fourth is outbound logistics and warehousing. We operate two distribution centers: a primary DC in Memphis, Tennessee, near the FedEx hub, managed by warehouse director Patricia Okafor, and a European DC in Rotterdam, Netherlands, operated by our third-party logistics partner, DHL Supply Chain. Memphis handles all North American fulfillment and ships globally. The Memphis DC is 180,000 square feet and uses an AutoStore automated storage and retrieval system installed by Swisslog in 2021.

Fifth is last-mile delivery. In North America, we use FedEx Freight for LTL shipments and FedEx Ground for parcel. For full truckload, we contract with XPO Logistics and Schneider National. In Europe, delivery is handled by DHL and DB Schenker. In Asia-Pacific, we use Kerry Logistics and Nippon Express. Our target delivery time is 5 business days for stocked items in North America and 10 business days for Europe.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: What were the major supply chain disruptions you faced recently?

The semiconductor shortage of 2021 to 2023 was by far the most severe disruption. We were heavily dependent on STMicroelectronics for a specific microcontroller, the STM32F4 series, used in both FlexCore and EdgeSense products. When lead times extended from 12 weeks to 52 weeks, our production at Guadalajara and Shenzhen was severely impacted. We lost an estimated 45 million dollars in revenue in fiscal year 2022 due to inability to fulfill orders.

Our response was multi-pronged. First, Lisa Yamamoto's procurement team qualified a second source — NXP Semiconductors' LPC5500 series — as a drop-in replacement. This qualification process took eight months and cost 1.2 million dollars in engineering redesign, led by our hardware engineering manager, Tom Bradley. Second, we increased strategic buffer inventory for all critical semiconductors from 60 days to 90 days. Third, our CFO Michael Torres approved a 15 million dollar advance purchase commitment with Texas Instruments for their next-generation AM6x microprocessor family to secure future allocation.

The Suez Canal blockage in March 2021 disrupted our inbound shipments from Shenzhen to Rotterdam for three weeks. Fourteen containers of EdgeSense modules were stuck on the Ever Given. Patricia Okafor coordinated emergency air freight shipments from Hong Kong to Frankfurt, costing us an additional 380,000 dollars. After this incident, we implemented a dual-routing policy: critical shipments from Asia now have a backup route via the Cape of Good Hope, with automatic route-switching triggers when Suez transit times exceed 15 days.

COVID-19 caused a two-week shutdown of our Guadalajara plant in April 2020 due to government lockdown orders. Carlos Mendoza worked with local health authorities to implement safety protocols that allowed a partial restart within ten days. The Shenzhen plant faced intermittent disruptions through late 2022 due to China's zero-COVID policies. Helen Wu managed this by building local component buffers and cross-training workers to maintain minimum viable production with reduced staff.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: What technology systems support your supply chain operations?

Our backbone is SAP S/4HANA, which we migrated to from SAP ECC in 2020. The migration project was a 14-month effort led by Angela Foster, our IT director, in partnership with Deloitte Consulting. Total cost was approximately 8.5 million dollars. SAP handles our MRP (Material Requirements Planning), production scheduling, inventory management, and financial integration across all three plants.

For supply chain visibility, we use FourKites for real-time shipment tracking. FourKites integrates with our carriers — Maersk, FedEx, DHL, XPO — and provides predicted ETAs with about 92 percent accuracy. For demand forecasting, Dr. Priya Krishnamurthy's data science team built a custom model using Prophet and LightGBM, deployed on Microsoft Azure. This model ingests historical sales data, macroeconomic indicators, and customer forecast submissions through our EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) connections to predict demand 12 months forward. Forecast accuracy has improved from 68 percent to 81 percent MAPE since deployment.

We use Coupa for procurement-to-pay processes and supplier management. Kinaxis RapidResponse is our supply chain planning tool for scenario modeling — for example, simulating the impact of a potential port strike or a sudden 20 percent increase in copper prices. Our quality management system runs on Siemens Opcenter Quality (formerly Camstar), which tracks defect rates, supplier quality metrics, and corrective actions. Current overall defect rate across all plants is 0.3 percent, well below our target of 0.5 percent.
