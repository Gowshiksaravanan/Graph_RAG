Interview with Sarah Mitchell - VP of Pricing Strategy, NovaTech Industries
Date: March 15, 2024
Location: NovaTech Headquarters, Chicago, Illinois

My name is Sarah Mitchell, and I've been leading the pricing strategy division at NovaTech Industries for the past six years. NovaTech is a mid-sized manufacturer of industrial automation components, headquartered in Chicago, Illinois. We operate three manufacturing plants — one in Detroit, Michigan, one in Guadalajara, Mexico, and one in Shenzhen, China. Our annual revenue is approximately 1.2 billion dollars, and we serve customers across North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How does NovaTech approach pricing for its product lines?

We have four main product lines: the ProLine series for heavy industrial automation, the FlexCore series for small and medium enterprise automation, the EdgeSense line of IoT sensor modules, and the newly launched GreenDrive series of energy-efficient motor controllers. Each product line has its own pricing model. For ProLine, we use a cost-plus pricing strategy with a target margin of 35 percent, because these are custom-engineered solutions where our customers — companies like Caterpillar, John Deere, and Siemens — expect transparent pricing tied to bill of materials. For FlexCore, we use competitive pricing benchmarked against our main competitors: Rockwell Automation, ABB, and Schneider Electric. We monitor their pricing quarterly through our market intelligence team led by David Chen.

The EdgeSense IoT modules use a tiered subscription pricing model. The hardware itself is sold near cost at around 12 percent margin, but the real revenue comes from the EdgeSense Cloud Platform, which charges per-device-per-month fees. We have three tiers: Basic at 4.99 dollars per device per month, Professional at 12.99, and Enterprise at 24.99 with custom SLA agreements. Our Chief Technology Officer, Raj Patel, pushed hard for this model because it creates recurring revenue. The GreenDrive series uses value-based pricing because the energy savings our customers realize are substantial — typically 18 to 25 percent reduction in energy costs. We price GreenDrive at a 15 percent premium over comparable non-green alternatives from competitors like Mitsubishi Electric and Yaskawa.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How do raw material costs affect your pricing decisions?

Raw material costs are a constant challenge. Our primary raw materials include copper, aluminum, rare earth magnets specifically neodymium, steel, and various semiconductor components. We source copper primarily from Freeport-McMoRan and Glencore. Aluminum comes from Alcoa and Rio Tinto. Neodymium magnets are sourced from Northern Minerals in Australia and Shenghe Resources in China. Semiconductor components come from Texas Instruments, STMicroelectronics, and NXP Semiconductors. Our procurement director, Lisa Yamamoto, manages relationships with over 200 suppliers globally.

When copper prices spiked by 28 percent in Q3 2023, we had to make a difficult decision. We absorbed the first 10 percent increase, then implemented a raw material surcharge mechanism that we call the Commodity Adjustment Factor or CAF. The CAF is reviewed monthly and applied to all ProLine and FlexCore orders. Our CFO, Michael Torres, insisted on this approach rather than permanent price increases because commodity prices are cyclical. The CAF formula is tied to the London Metal Exchange copper index and the Shanghai Futures Exchange aluminum index. For semiconductor components, we maintain a strategic buffer inventory of 90 days for critical parts, managed through our SAP S/4HANA ERP system.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: How do you handle pricing for different geographic regions?

Regional pricing is complex. In North America, which accounts for 55 percent of our revenue, we price in US dollars and offer standard Net-30 payment terms, with a 2 percent discount for Net-10. In Europe, where we generate about 25 percent of revenue, we price in euros through our subsidiary NovaTech Europe GmbH based in Munich, Germany. Our European pricing is typically 8 to 12 percent higher than North America due to CE marking compliance costs, the WEEE directive recycling fees, and VAT considerations. We work with our European distribution partner, Distrelec, for smaller accounts.

In Asia-Pacific, contributing 20 percent of revenue, we have different strategies by country. In Japan, we work exclusively through our distribution partner Misumi Group. In China, our Shenzhen facility sells direct through NovaTech China Ltd., and pricing is 15 to 20 percent lower than North America because local manufacturing costs are lower and competition from domestic manufacturers like Inovance Technology and Estun Automation is intense. In India, we recently partnered with Tata AutoComp as our distribution and integration partner. Our Regional Pricing Committee, which I chair along with regional sales directors Marcus Weber for Europe, Kenji Tanaka for Japan, and Wei Zhang for China, meets monthly to review regional pricing adjustments.

INTERVIEWER QUESTION: What pricing tools and analytics do you use?

We invested heavily in pricing analytics over the past three years. Our primary tool is PROS Holdings' pricing optimization platform, which we integrated with our Salesforce CRM in January 2023. The integration project was led by our IT director, Angela Foster, and cost approximately 2.4 million dollars. PROS uses machine learning algorithms to analyze win-loss data, customer willingness to pay, and competitive positioning to recommend optimal price points. Since implementing PROS, our average deal margin has improved by 3.2 percentage points.

We also use Vendavo for contract pricing management, particularly for our top 50 enterprise accounts. These accounts — including General Electric, Toyota Motor Corporation, Volkswagen Group, Samsung Electronics, and Boeing — represent about 40 percent of our total revenue. Each enterprise account has a dedicated pricing agreement negotiated annually by our key account managers. Our data science team, led by Dr. Priya Krishnamurthy, built a custom price elasticity model using Python and deployed it on AWS SageMaker. This model processes point-of-sale data from our distribution partners and predicts volume impact of price changes with about 85 percent accuracy.
